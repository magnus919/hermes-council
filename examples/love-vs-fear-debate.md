SYNTHESIS

  Points of Consensus:
    • Timescale matters: fear dominates short-term/immediate response,
      love dominates long-term/sustainable outcomes.
    • Context determines which emotion is more "powerful" — crisis vs
      stability, individual vs society, immediate vs generational.
    • The definition of 'power' is central to any answer and must be
      specified before comparison.

  Points of Divergence:
    • Whether the question is meaningful at all (Aisha's meta-position
      vs the others' willingness to engage).
    • Whether fear has a universal biological basis that transcends
      cultural context (Marcus, Leif vs Yuki).
    • Whether love's long-term benefits outweigh fear's immediate potency
      (Elena vs Marcus, Leif).

  Key Insights:
    • The most productive insight was Elena's recognition that the debate
      was using 'power' in four distinct senses simultaneously — a
      metacognitive breakthrough that emerged from the cross-examination.
    • Aisha's low-confidence (0.15) contrarian position served as an
      essential forcing function, making every other agent sharpen their
      definitions.
    • Every agent's confidence either decreased or stayed flat — no one
      became more certain after reading others. This is a healthy signal
      of genuine engagement rather than polarization.
    • Yuki's observation that love and fear interact rather than compete
      — love can mitigate fear, fear can motivate protective love — was
      the most novel frame introduced.

  Recommendation:
    Acknowledge the question's framing problem first. Then answer for
    a specified domain: fear has more power over milliseconds to minutes,
    love has more power over years to generations. Neither is universally
    more powerful; they operate on different timescales and in different
    contexts, and they interact in ways that make a unidimensional
    comparison fundamentally incomplete.
